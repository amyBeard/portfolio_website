Work Term Reports

Avatara Software

Avatara Software is a software development company focusing on web-based management systems primarily for Canada's labour and nuclear industries. Their systems offer solutions to these unions and businesses that are tailor-made to their specific requirements. With only 4 full-time employees, this company has had an impressive impact on the market, taking on massive clients such as Black & McDonald, the IBEW, and E.S. Fox Ltd. As a small company, they’ve made their name in this industry through their ability to form close relationships with their clients and deliver functional, effective products consistently and quickly.

Avatara Software primarily focuses on four types of software systems; quality management, training compliance management, market recovery/STAB, and apprentice management. Avatara creates custom web management tools to help its clients simplify and organize their current systems. Their goal is to make the lives and jobs of their clients easier through these well-made, easy-to-use systems.

What sets Avatara apart from the rest is its client-based approach to its development process. Not only acknowledging what the customer wants but fully comprehending the problem the client has, allows the company to better understand the implicit needs of their customers. This allows them to create incredibly tailored solutions that are intuitive to the user. Furthermore, their process of constantly returning to the client during development and receiving their input to improve the product further aids in creating an end-product the client loves.

My Role & Experience
Summer 2024

As a full-stack developer, this term I worked on an array of different projects, continuously switching from back-end to front-end. As an introductory project, I worked with another co-op student to update the company’s in-house, time-tracking software to a new PHP framework. This project allowed me to simultaneously learn Laravel, MySQL, and Codeigniter while learning about the company’s development standards and best practices. As work on that project slowed, I was pulled into numerous other projects ranging from action-tracking systems for Black and McDonald to a materials management system for E.S. Fox Ltd.

However, my primary focus this term was on an apprentice tracking system for IBEW Local 586. The goal of this project was to provide a new user interface for the apprentices to view their progress, as well as incorporate a new apprentice stream into their pre-existing system. My main contribution to this project was the front-end portion of the new UI for the apprentices. I was tasked with building new pages where the apprentices could log in to view their data and progress within their respective streams. This project allowed me to further my understanding of the MVC design structure, as well as front-end packages like Bootstrap 5 and jQuery. This project helped me to refine my pre-existing front-end development skills and later gain more experience with back- end development.

Throughout this term, I gained valuable insights into the company’s business. Working on the time-tracking software and engaging with colleagues at conventions helped me understand how the business operates and what drives its success. Avatara’s ability to build lasting relationships with clients has allowed their business to prosper, thanks to its reputation and reliability. It was incredible to witness clients enthusiastically promote Avatara’s systems to their peers at conventions, almost as if they were pitching the product themselves. This experience highlighted for me not only the importance of creating effective systems but also how strong, meaningful relationships with clients are key to sustained business growth.

In all, this first term with Avatara has been incredibly informative, allowing me to learn and develop so many unique skills I would have never gained elsewhere. I came into this term with goals of learning more about the software development industry, improving my professional collaboration skills, and becoming an all-around better programmer. It is safe to say that during this term I have achieved all these goals—and more. The team at Avatara has been supportive throughout, encouraging me to take on new challenges, try different approaches, and push myself to grow. I’m eager to continue building on what I’ve learned and take on new opportunities for growth in the future.

Summer 2025

This summer I completed my third full-stack developer co-op with Avatara Software. Each term with this team has challenged me in new ways, and this one was no exception. I spent the semester moving up and down the stack—designing database schemas, writing import tools, building a reusable package, and fitting new workflows into existing apps. The result was a term that felt both challenging and coherent: a series of projects that sharpened my judgment while reinforcing habits I've been building around documentation, independent problem-solving, and thoughtful pacing.

Being on a small team means seeing projects end-to-end: meeting with clients to understand what they need, sketching a plan, building it, reviewing it, and polishing the details so it holds up after it ships. It also means your decisions matter; the way you shape a feature today becomes someone else's daily workflow tomorrow. That sense of responsibility guided how I worked this term.

One of my larger projects was moving a client's annotated records from spreadsheets into our system for a major Canadian nuclear-industry partner. I built a tool that gathers the information, tidies it up, and imports it safely, with checks so we can validate the importer's effectiveness. The most valuable part was working closely with the client to agree on what each field meant and how to handle the odd cases. The project taught me about the practical challenges of data migration and the nuances of extracting and importing data across different formats and platforms.

I also helped turn a paper-and-email purchasing workflow into software by contributing a purchase requisition module for Black & McDonald's material-management system. The goal was to fold requisitions and approvals into the existing application without disrupting what already worked. The most interesting piece was approvals sequencing: I implemented manual reordering of approvers using DataTables RowReorder so users could drag to set the approval path, then connected that to back-end logic that saves the sequence, enforces rules, and keeps the flow consistent. With steady reviews from my supervisor, I worked through the integration details and delivered the module successfully.

My last key project changed how I think about building software. I created a small package that plugs into one of our existing packages to add a feature without changing the original. The "meta" nature of that work felt tricky at first, but working with my supervisor and a teammate helped me make the setup clear and the package easy to adopt. I focused on straightforward setup steps, efficient integration, and solid documentation so teams could enable it without touching internals. The extra time I spent on self-review paid off: code review came back with only minor notes, reinforcing that slower, more deliberate work leads to smoother integrations.

At the start of the term I set a handful of goals, and they shaped how I approached my days. I wanted to deepen my understanding of the lower-level parts of our stack and rely less on quick searches or old snippets. Practically, that meant spending time with official documentation and building mental models I could carry from project to project. The payoff showed up in small but important moments—like diagnosing and fixing a tricky import issue on my own using our database tools. It was a good reminder that investing in fundamentals makes the rest of the work calmer.

I also aimed to lead with my own solution before checking how teammates had done it in the past. I still validate against team patterns, but starting with my own plan changed the tone of reviews from "does this match?" to "is this the best trade-off?" That shift was especially helpful on the package work.

Another focus was writing. Outside of work I took a history-on-film course with weekly responses and quick feedback, and that steady practice carried over into my READMEs and commenting. I started drafting earlier, revising more intentionally, and writing with the reader's questions in mind. The result was clearer documentation that will aid future developers.

Finally, I worked on pacing. I tend to sprint when I'm excited, which can create avoidable rework. This term I stuck to a self-review routine—blocking time to re-read and test my code before shipping—and it made a real difference. Tickets came back less often and with smaller changes.

Overall, the projects I worked on this term covered a broad range of topics and pushed my skills further than I expected. With each new project, I felt grateful for the opportunity to learn more.

I'm grateful to the Avatara team for thoughtful reviews, generous mentorship, and the trust to learn by doing. I'm also thankful to our clients for collaborating closely during migrations and workflow design; those conversations make the software better. Next term, I'm excited to keep building at the intersection of engineering and usability—deepening my understanding of the stack, taking on more package-level and architectural work, and continuing the habits that made this summer both productive and sustainable.
